Chapter Two

The Tide Comes In

The water came back the way it always did, without apology, filling the channels it had emptied at dawn. Mara watched from the sea wall as the mud lost its sheen and became, again, a thing that could drown you.

She had counted on three more hours. The almanac had promised them, and her father had sworn by the almanac the way other men swore by scripture. But the marsh kept its own calendar, and today it had decided to be early.

The harbour

Down in the harbour the boats were already lifting, straining at their lines like animals that smelled a storm. Old Crewe was out on the slip, shouting at no one, which meant he was frightened.

"You don't argue with the tide, girl. You only learn how long you've got."

It was the truest thing he ever said to her, and he said it the way he said everything — as if it cost him nothing. Mara would remember it years later, in a city with no water at all, and understand finally what it had cost.
